Understanding Australian Migration

This interactive dashboard examines two decades of migration data (2004‚Äì2023), revealing how Australia's intake has evolved through economic cycles, policy changes, and the COVID-19 pandemic.

Key concepts:

  • Net Overseas Migration (NOM): The difference between people arriving to live in Australia (immigrants) and people departing to live overseas (emigrants). A positive number means more arrivals than departures.
  • Visa types: Australian migration is categorized by visa purpose - Skilled (work-based), Family (reunification), Student (education), Humanitarian (refugees), and Visitor/Working Holiday (temporary).
  • Australian-born vs Overseas-born: Refers to whether a migrant is an Australian citizen returning from abroad, or someone born in another country moving to Australia.
  • GFC (Global Financial Crisis): The 2008-2009 worldwide economic recession that impacted migration patterns.

Use the interactive sliders and click on legends to explore specific years, visa types, and countries. Hover over any chart element to see detailed data.

Now that we've seen the overall trend, let's understand why migrants come. This chart breaks down migration by visa category, revealing the policy levers that shape Australia's intake.

The Cumulative Impact: Australia's Diverse Population

Australia's Population by Country of Birth, 2024

üí° Hover over segments to see exact population figures and percentages

This visualisation shows the cumulative result of decades of migration policy. This snapshot of Australia's resident population by country of birth as of 2024 captures how migration has transformed the nation.

While Australian-born residents still form the majority, the overseas-born share has grown substantially. The UK remains the largest single overseas birthplace, but Asian countries now collectively represent a major proportion of the population.

Population composition:
  • Nearly 30% overseas-born: Australia ranks among the world's most culturally diverse nations
  • UK historical dominance: Despite declining recent migration, the UK remains the top overseas birthplace due to historical waves
  • Asian growth: India, China, Philippines, and Vietnam are rapidly increasing their representation
  • Generational shift: Second-generation Australians (children of migrants) are counted as Australian-born, meaning the true migrant heritage is even larger

Summary & Key Insights

What the data tells us

Australia's migration system has undergone profound transformation over the past two decades. The shift from European to Asian source countries, the rise of student migration as a dominant pathway, and the system's resilience through economic and health crises all point to migration's central role in Australia's economic and demographic future.

Skilled and student migration remain Australia's largest entry streams, driving population growth concentrated in major cities ‚Äî particularly Sydney and Melbourne. This shapes labour markets, housing demand, and cultural diversity.

Questions for further exploration

This dashboard raises important questions for policymakers and researchers:

  • How can regional Australia better attract and retain migrants?
  • What industries benefit most from skilled migration, and are there mismatches between visa settings and labour needs?
  • How do monthly migration flows differ from annual trends, and what does this reveal about seasonal patterns?
  • What role does return migration play, and why do so many Australian-born residents leave?

Data limitations: Some categories are grouped for clarity. Financial-year aggregation may obscure monthly volatility. Temporary visa holders who later gain permanent residency may be double-counted across categories.
